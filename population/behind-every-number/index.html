The Scale of the Crisis

Nearly 55,000 Adults. Three Years. One County.

Between 2023 and 2025, nearly 55,000 adults aged 18–59 accessed homeless services in Alameda County. These are not statistics — they are neighbors, family members, and community members who needed a safe place to sleep, eat, and heal. More than a third had a diagnosed mental health condition, and nearly half carried some form of disability. The scale of compounding need is immense.

Racial Inequity in Homelessness

Who Is Most Affected

Homelessness does not fall equally across communities. Black and African American residents represent 31.1% of all homeless individuals in Alameda County’s system, despite being a far smaller share of the county’s general population. This is a racial equity crisis as much as a housing crisis, and any lasting solution must address both.

Mental Illness & Homelessness

In Every Age Group, More Than Half

Mental illness among people experiencing homelessness in Alameda County is not confined to a single demographic. In every age cohort from 18 to 64, more than 57% of homeless individuals carry a mental health condition. It is the defining common thread running through the entire system — which is why SHCLA’s model is not a one-time intervention but a systemic response.

The Housing Gap

Permanent Supportive Housing Is Mental Health Housing

72.2%

of all individuals who accessed a Permanent Supportive Housing (PSH) program in Alameda County across 2023–2025 had a diagnosed mental health condition.

PSH is not a generic housing program — it is, in practice, a mental health intervention. Yet the supply of PSH falls far short of the need. SHCLA’s model — acquiring land permanently through a community land trust, master-leasing to licensed operators, and keeping homes affordable forever — is built precisely to fill this gap in a way that market forces alone cannot.

SHCLA Exists Because the Facts and Experiences Demand It

Nearly 20,000 people in Alameda County experienced homelessness alongside a serious mental illness between 2023 and 2025. More than 13,000 were chronically homeless. Thousands had no income, no stability, no foothold. SHCLA’s model of permanent community land trust housing is a carefully designed, data-grounded response to a crisis that the market cannot solve alone.
